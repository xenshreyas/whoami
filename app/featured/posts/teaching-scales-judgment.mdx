---
title: 'Teaching Scales Judgment'
publishedAt: '2025-12-08'
summary: 'Teaching DSA and operating systems made me better at engineering because it forced me to explain the ideas I normally take for granted.'
---

Teaching changed how I think about software.

For two years I have taught data structures, algorithms, and operating systems. I started because I wanted to give back to students who were roughly where I used to be. I kept doing it because it turned out to be one of the best ways to improve my own judgment.

When you teach, vague understanding gets exposed immediately. You cannot hide behind intuition for long. If a student asks *why does this invariant matter?* or *why is this synchronization bug subtle?*, you either have a coherent answer or you do not.

That pressure is useful.

It forces you to separate:

- what is fundamental from what is incidental,
- what beginners need now from what can wait,
- and what explanation is actually doing the work.

I think that maps directly to engineering. Good code review, good API design, and good systems work all depend on being able to tell the difference between surface complexity and structural complexity.

Teaching also made me more patient. Most people do not need more information. They need the right framing, in the right order, with the right level of precision.

That is true in the classroom and in software teams.
