---
title: 'Startups Reward Compression'
publishedAt: '2026-01-12'
summary: 'What I like about startup environments is not chaos. It is compression: tighter loops between decision, implementation, and reality.'
---

The best startup environments do not feel random. They feel compressed.

By compression I mean that the distance between *idea*, *build*, *feedback*, and *revision* gets dramatically shorter. A weak assumption does not survive for long. A good idea is not trapped in planning documents for weeks. You learn faster because the system forces contact with reality.

That is a big part of why I enjoy early-stage work. It is not because I want to move fast for its own sake. Plenty of teams move quickly and still waste time. What matters is whether the loop is tight enough to produce better judgment.

A compressed environment changes what you optimize for:

- You write software that is legible enough to change tomorrow.
- You ask narrower questions and get real answers sooner.
- You care more about whether users pull on the product than whether the internal story sounds elegant.

It also changes what good engineering looks like. In a mature organization, excellence often means reducing variance. In a startup, excellence often means reducing time-to-learning without turning the codebase into debt.

That balance is hard, and it is exactly why the work is interesting.
